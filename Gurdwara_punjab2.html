Sarai Amanat Khan

#sarai #mughal #calligraphy #brick #caravanserai #civilengineering

Location & Period

Location: Amritsar District, Punjab (near Attari)
Dynasty/Period: Mughal Period, early 17th century (c. 1640 CE)

History

  • Named after Amanat Khan, the royal calligrapher of the Taj Mahal, who settled here after retirement.
  • Built as a caravanserai for traders and travelers along the Lahore–Delhi route of the Grand Trunk Road.
  • Served both as a resting place and checkpoint for travelers during the Mughal period.

Architecture

  • Square-shaped walled enclosure with four corner bastions.
  • Grand entrance gateway with detailed Persian-style calligraphy and ornamental arches.
  • Cells (hujras) for lodging built around a large central courtyard.
  • Includes a mosque, well, and small garden areas inside.

Civil Engineering & Materials

  • Constructed with Lakhori bricks and lime mortar.
  • Use of red sandstone panels in the main gate for decoration.
  • Raised plinth, drainage channels, and rainwater collection system.
  • Thick walls for thermal insulation and security.

Builders’ Thought Process

  • Built for comfort and protection of travelers on imperial roads.
  • Materials chosen for local availability and climatic suitability.
  • Gateway designed to reflect royal aesthetic and Amanat Khan’s calligraphic legacy.
  • Functional architecture with centralized layout for surveillance and maintenance.

Modern Comparison

Unique Features

  • Calligraphic gateway—a rare public work signed by Amanat Khan himself.
  • Among the few surviving Mughal serais in near-original condition.
  • Combines Mughal aesthetics with utilitarian planning.
