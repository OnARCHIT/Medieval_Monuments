Jamia Masjid, Srinagar

#mosque #kashmirarchitecture #timberarchitecture #medievalkashmir #courtyardmosque

Location & Period

Location: Nowhatta, Srinagar, Jammu & Kashmir
Dynasty/Period: Sultan Sikandar (built in 1402 CE), expanded by Zain-ul-Abidin
Religion: Islam (Sunni)

History

  • Commissioned by Sultan Sikandar in 1402 CE during the Shah Mir Dynasty.
  • Enlarged by Zain-ul-Abidin, his son and one of the most tolerant rulers in Kashmir.
  • It survived several fires and was rebuilt each time—final major restoration in the 1670s.
  • A center for religious discourse, political movement, and Islamic scholarship in Kashmir.

Architecture

  • Classical quadrangular courtyard plan with three large entrances.
  • Surrounded by arcaded cloisters and an open central prayer hall.
  • The prayer hall has 378 wooden Deodar pillars, each over 20 ft high.
  • Indo-Islamic and Persian architectural fusion with wooden roofs, pagoda-style domes, and chinar tree inside the courtyard.

Civil Engineering & Materials

  • Deodar wood (Himalayan cedar) used extensively for pillars and roofing.
  • Burnt bricks, lime mortar, and dressed stone used for base and walls.
  • Timber trusses support large pitched roofs—climate adaptive.
  • Raised platform ensures protection from snow and flooding.

Materials Properties

Material Properties
Deodar Wood Highly durable, insect-resistant, flexible under seismic loads
Burnt Brick Strong in compression, heat-insulating
Lime Mortar Breathable, allows minor movements, long life expectancy

Reason for Material Selection

  • Deodar wood was abundant, resistant to insects and snow-load pressure.
  • Timber allowed flexibility and seismic adaptation in an earthquake-prone zone.
  • Burnt brick and lime were traditional Kashmiri materials suited for thermal variation.

Structural Design and Engineering

  • Post-and-beam system with closely spaced columns supporting heavy roof trusses.
  • Pitched roofs avoid snow accumulation and reduce pressure on structure.
  • Brick foundation and elevated plinth provide moisture protection.
  • Central chinar tree and open space support natural light and ventilation.

Stability Features

  • 378 timber columns distribute roof load uniformly.
  • Timber joints allow for shock absorption during earthquakes.
  • Pitched roofs and wide eaves efficiently manage rain and snow.
  • Fire-prone material (wood) balanced by spaced layout and periodic maintenance.

Builders’ Thought Process

  • Inspired by Persian courtyard mosque architecture, adapted to local Himalayan context.
  • Emphasis on simplicity, durability, and communal scale.
  • Local craftsmanship and materials used to align with Islamic aesthetic and spiritual values.
  • Designed for cold climate—closed hall with high roofs for insulation.

Unique Features

  • Houses one of the largest prayer halls in Kashmir, over 1,000 m².
  • Chinar tree inside mosque courtyard—symbol of Kashmir.
  • One of the few mosques with purely wooden Indo-Islamic architecture.
  • Survived multiple reconstructions and still remains a religious and political symbol.
