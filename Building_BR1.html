Nalanda University Ruins

Nalanda District, Bihar, India — UNESCO World Heritage Site

Location Nalanda, Nalanda District, Bihar, India
25.1368° N, 85.4437° E
Dynasty Gupta Empire (5th century CE, with Pala additions)
Deity Buddhist (Center of learning)

Historical Background

Founded in the 5th century CE, Nalanda was a premier Buddhist university, flourishing under Gupta and Pala patronage until its decline in the 12th century. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2016, it housed thousands of scholars and texts. The Sariputra Stupa, built by Ashoka, commemorates Buddha’s disciple.
References: Thrillophilia, Tripadvisor, Wikipedia, UNESCO

Architecture

  • Ruins feature monasteries, stupas, and lecture halls in a grid layout.
  • The Sariputra Stupa is a stepped pyramid with intricate brickwork, reflecting Manasara principles of spatial organization.
  • Monasteries (viharas) are oblong brick structures with cells around a courtyard; shrines and stupas stand in front.
  • Site covers about 23 hectares, with 11 viharas and 14 temples unearthed.
    Reference: R.N. Misra, 2016

Building Materials & Properties

  • Brick: Primary material, locally sourced (~10–20 MPa strength), high porosity, suited for plastering.
  • Lime-Surkhi Mortar: Binder:aggregate ratio ~1:2, with surkhi and organic additives (jaggery, black gram); hydraulic, breathable, durable in humid climates.

Reference: Ngoma, 2009

Builder Thought Process

Gupta and Pala rulers aimed to create an intellectual hub, using brick for its affordability and lime-surkhi mortar for durability. The grid layout followed Manasara for efficient space use and spiritual harmony.

Structural Design & Current Condition

  • Load-bearing brick masonry with thick walls and vaults.
  • Sariputra Stupa’s stepped design enhances stability.
  • Currently in ruins due to 12th-century invasions; ASI preserves exposed brickwork with lime-based repairs.

Reference: INTACH Heritage at Risk Register

Comparison: Nalanda (Lime-Masonry) vs. RCC Structures

Aspect Nalanda (Lime-Masonry) RCC Structures
Material Brick, lime-surkhi mortar Concrete, steel
Breathability High (prevents moisture damage) Low (prone to moisture damage)
Strength Moderate (10–20 MPa) High (20–50 MPa)
Elasticity Flexible, absorbs stress Rigid, prone to cracking
Durability High (centuries) Moderate (100–150 years)

Lime mortar’s elasticity and breathability made Nalanda’s structures resilient in Bihar’s climate, while RCC offers greater strength but less flexibility.

Unique Features

  • Sariputra Stupa: Stepped pyramid design, housing relics.
  • Grid Layout: Planned academic complex, rare for its time.
  • Viharas: Residential-educational buildings with cells around courtyards.
