Historical Background

Built between 1540–1545 CE by architect Alawal Khan, the Sher Shah Suri Tomb is an Indo-Islamic masterpiece often called the “Second Taj Mahal.” Set in the center of an artificial lake, it commemorates the founder of the Suri Empire. The tomb was completed shortly after Sher Shah Suri’s death and is listed as a monument of national importance.
References: Outlook Traveller, Tusktravel, Wikipedia

Architecture

  • Three-storied red sandstone mausoleum (122 ft tall) on an octagonal plan, surmounted by a large dome.
  • Surrounded by an artificial lake, accessible by a stone causeway.
  • Features chhatris (domed kiosks) at each corner, arched verandahs, and open courtyards.
  • Blends Afghan and Mughal styles, with symmetry influenced by Manasara treatises.
  • Interior is octagonal, with arched windows and latticed screens illuminating the chamber. The western wall contains a decorated mihrab for prayer.

Reference: R.N. Misra, 2016

Building Materials & Properties

  • Red Sandstone: Sourced locally (~40–60 MPa strength, ~5–8% porosity), durable and suitable for carving.
  • Lime-Surkhi Mortar: Binder:aggregate ratio ~1:2, with surkhi and organic additives; hydraulic, breathable, ideal for humid conditions.

References: Ngoma, 2009, Raheem & Tahir, 2008

Builder Thought Process

Architect Alawal Khan aimed for grandeur and durability, selecting red sandstone for its strength and beauty. The artificial lake not only enhanced the monument’s aesthetics but also contributed to foundation stability. Lime-surkhi mortar ensured resilience in Bihar’s humid climate, while the octagonal plan and domed structure followed Indo-Islamic and Manasara principles.

Structural Design & Current Condition

  • Load-bearing sandstone masonry with a central dome and deep foundations.
  • The lake reduces soil pressure and risk of settlement.
  • Well-preserved; minor weathering addressed by ASI using lime-based repairs.

Reference: INTACH Heritage at Risk Register

Breathability High (prevents moisture damage) Low (prone to moisture damage)

Lime mortar’s porosity and flexibility have helped preserve the tomb for centuries, while RCC structures, though stronger, are less resilient to moisture and time.

Unique Features

  • Artificial Lake: Enhances aesthetics and structural stability.
  • Indo-Islamic Design: Blends Afghan and Mughal elements; octagonal plan with domed kiosks (chhatris).
  • Largest Dome: At the time of completion, the dome was the largest in India.
