Allahabad Fort, Prayagraj

Location: Prayagraj (Allahabad), Uttar Pradesh, India
Coordinates: 25.4314° N, 81.8756° E
Dynasty: Mughal | Built by: Emperor Akbar, 1583 CE

Allahabad Fort on the Yamuna riverbank (Wikimedia Commons)

Historical Background

Commissioned by Emperor Akbar in 1583 at the Triveni Sangam (confluence of Ganges, Yamuna, and mythical Saraswati), Allahabad Fort was a strategic Mughal stronghold and administrative center.
The fort reflects a synthesis of Mughal and Hindu architectural elements, described as an "Architectural Triveni" by R.N. Misra (2016), highlighting its cultural and spiritual significance.
Reference: R.N. Misra, 2016; Wikipedia

Architecture

  • Massive red sandstone walls (20–25 m high), three gateways, defensive bastions
  • Key structures: Zenana Palace, Saraswati Koop, relocated Ashoka Pillar (3rd century BCE)
  • Design aligns with Manasara principles of fortification and spatial harmony
  • Blend of Mughal arches and Hindu motifs
  • Fort once had 25 gateways, 23 bastions, and multiple palaces and halls
References: R.N. Misra, 2016; Indianpanorama; Agate Travel; Max Hotels; Tripoto; IJEMH (2023)

Building Materials & Properties

  • Red Sandstone: Sourced from Chunar, used for walls and arches (40–60 MPa compressive strength, 5–8% porosity)
  • Lime-Surkhi Mortar: Binder:aggregate ~1:2, with surkhi (crushed brick), jaggery, hemp fiber for hydraulic properties
  • Brick: Used in internal structures and later additions
  • Properties: Sandstone is durable and carvable; lime-surkhi mortar is hydraulic, breathable, and manages moisture well
References: Ngoma, 2009; Raheem & Tahir, 2008

Builder Thought Process

Akbar aimed to create a fortified administrative center at a sacred Hindu site, integrating Mughal and Hindu aesthetics per Manasara guidelines. Chunar sandstone was chosen for durability and proximity, while lime-surkhi mortar ensured flexibility in the humid climate—reflecting strategic and cultural vision.
Reference: R.N. Misra, 2016

Structural Design & Current Condition

  • Load-bearing sandstone masonry with thick walls and deep foundations to counter riverbank erosion
  • Arched gateways and bastions enhance stability
  • Partially stable; restricted access due to military use
  • Weathering and incompatible cement repairs have caused efflorescence; ASI now uses lime-based repairs
Reference: INTACH Heritage at Risk Register

Comparison with RCC Structures

  • Allahabad Fort (Lime-Masonry): Flexible, breathable, suited to humid conditions, reducing cracks
  • RCC Structures: Higher strength (20–50 MPa vs. ~10 MPa for lime mortar), but rigid and less breathable, prone to moisture damage
  • Advantage: Lime mortar’s porosity prevents water retention, unlike RCC’s impermeability
Reference: Ngoma, 2009

Unique Features

  • Triveni Sangam Location: Strategic and sacred site at the river confluence
  • Ashoka Pillar: Relocated Mauryan pillar with inscriptions, symbolizing historical continuity
  • Patalpuri Temple & Akshayavat Tree: Ancient underground shrine and immortal banyan tree within the fort
