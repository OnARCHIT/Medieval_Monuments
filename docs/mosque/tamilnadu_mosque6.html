Triplicane Big Mosque (Wallajah Mosque), Chennai

#mosque #mughal #granite #civilengineering

Location & Founder

Location: Triplicane, Chennai
Built: 1795 CE
Founder: Muhammad Ali Khan Wallajah family (Nawab of Arcot)

History

  • Constructed by the Nawab of Arcot’s family as a symbol of Mughal influence in South India[1][2][3][5][6][7][8][9][10].
  • Known for fostering Hindu-Muslim unity—a Hindu Munshi wrote the Persian chronogram at the entrance[1][3][4][5][9].
  • Served as a refuge for the Nawab during the Carnatic Wars and has witnessed centuries of Chennai’s history[2].

Architecture

  • Style: Mughal with Indo-Saracenic influences, featuring two gold-plated domes and two minarets (40m high)[1][2][4][5][6][7][8][9][10].
  • Materials: Entirely granite—no iron or wood used in the structure[1][2][4][5][6][7][8][9][10].
  • Plan: Large prayer hall, tank, spacious courtyard, mausoleums, and a Persian chronogram at the entrance[1][2][4][5][6][7][8][9][10].
  • Administration: Prince of Arcot Endowments Trust; most staff are Hindus, reflecting peaceful coexistence[1][4][5][9].

Civil Engineering & Materials

  • Granite: Chosen for strength, longevity, and resistance to termites and humidity[1][2][4][5][6][8][9][10].
  • No metal or wood: Avoids corrosion and decay in the coastal climate[1][2][4][5][6][8][9][10].
  • Foundation: Deep, supporting the heavy granite superstructure[1].

Builders’ Thought Process

  • Granite for permanence and grandeur; designed for scale and Mughal symbolism[1][2][4][5][6][8][9][10].
  • Large courtyard and tank for community gatherings and ritual purification[1][2][4][5][6][8][9][10].

Unique Features

  • Largest and oldest mosque in Chennai; can accommodate thousands of worshippers[1][2][4][5][6][8][9][10].
  • Symbol of peaceful coexistence (Hindu staff, Persian chronogram by a Hindu)[1][3][4][5][9].
  • Grave of Qaid-e-Millat M. Muhammad Ismail Sahib and Persian scholar Barool on premises[4][5][9].
  • Active worship site and landmark for the city[1][2][4][5][6][8][9][10].
