Kareng Ghar

#ahom #palace #brickarchitecture #assam #militaryarchitecture

Location & Period

Location: Garhgaon, near Sivasagar, Assam
Dynasty/Period: Ahom Dynasty, built originally in the 17th century, rebuilt in 1752 CE by King Rajeswar Singha

History

  • Originally constructed by Ahom king Suklenmung in the mid-16th century.
  • Destroyed and reconstructed in 1752 CE by King Rajeswar Singha using bricks.
  • Served as the principal royal palace before the capital shifted to Rangpur (Sivasagar).

Architecture

  • Multi-storeyed structure with arched doorways, vaulted halls, secret chambers, and watch towers.
  • Consists of four levels, including rooms for administration, court, and royal living.
  • Thick brick walls and decorative niches reflect Ahom adaptations of Mughal architecture.
  • Integrated into the Garhgaon Fort complex, surrounded by a 5 km-long wall and moat.

Civil Engineering & Materials

  • Constructed using burnt bricks bound with traditional Ahom mortar (lime, molasses, and sometimes egg whites).
  • Large vaulted roofs and corbelled arches provide structural integrity.
  • Use of natural ventilation and thermal mass in thick brick walls for climate control.
  • Moisture control and water-resistant construction essential due to Assam’s high rainfall.

Builders’ Thought Process

  • Shift from timber to brick for fire resistance and longevity.
  • Chose height and internal compartmentalization for administration, security, and climate comfort.
  • Strategic location in the plains of Assam ensured proximity to resources and central control.
  • Focused on fortification, multi-use royal functionality, and regional aesthetics.

Unique Features

  • A rare example of a multi-storeyed palace-fort in eastern India.
  • Blends residential, administrative, and defensive functions in a single structure.
  • Survives as a testament to the Ahom Empire’s architectural evolution from bamboo to brick.
  • One of the oldest and largest brick palaces in Assam still standing.
