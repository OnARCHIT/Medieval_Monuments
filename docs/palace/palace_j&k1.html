Pari Mahal

#mughalarchitecture #islamicscience #observatory #stone #terracedgardens #heritagestructure

Location & Patron

Location: Zabarwan Range, Srinagar, Jammu & Kashmir
Patron/Dynasty: Dara Shikoh (Mughal Prince, son of Shah Jahan) – 1650s
Function: Madrasa (Islamic school), observatory

History

  • Built in the mid-17th century by Prince Dara Shikoh, a Sufi scholar and eldest son of Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan.
  • Originally used as a madrasa (Islamic school) for teaching astronomy, mathematics, and astrology under Sufi guidance.
  • Associated with the Qadiri order of Sufism and Persianate culture.
  • Served partly as an observatory and garden palace.

Architecture

  • Six descending terraces carved into the Zabarwan hillside overlooking Dal Lake.
  • Mughal archways, stucco plaster, and garden layouts with Persian influences.
  • Absence of typical domes or minarets—blending fortress, garden, and academic architecture.
  • Designed to maximize sky visibility for astronomical purposes.

Civil Engineering & Materials

  • Constructed mainly using limestone rubble masonry with lime mortar.
  • Stucco finishing on walls, arched colonnades, and vaulted ceilings.
  • Terraces constructed with retaining walls to manage slope and prevent erosion.
  • Stone staircases and aqueducts for water management in the terraced garden.

Materials Properties

Material Properties
Limestone Durable, workable, suited for regional climate
Lime mortar Breathable, flexible, improves strength over time
Stucco Weather-resistant, reflective, allows smooth ornamentation

Reason for Material Selection

  • Locally available limestone ideal for the sloped terrain.
  • Lime mortar and stucco enhanced structural bonding and aesthetic finish.
  • Terrace construction and retaining walls required strong, compressive materials.
  • Focus on astronomy and education influenced the open layout with sky visibility.

Structural Design and Engineering

  • Terraced layout reduced structural load per level and enhanced stability on slope.
  • Arched halls and thick walls provided insulation and structural rigidity.
  • Designed with viewing angles for celestial bodies—aligning science and space.
  • Water channels and aqueducts supplied upper terraces—Mughal hydraulic engineering.

Stability Features

  • Retaining walls anchor terraces securely on steep terrain.
  • Vaulted halls and arched openings distribute seismic and wind loads evenly.
  • Masonry construction combined with lime mortar provides long-term durability.
  • Natural stone base reduces thermal fluctuation impacts.

Builders’ Thought Process

  • Aimed to create a spiritual and scientific retreat blending nature, knowledge, and Mughal garden aesthetics.
  • Emphasized sky observation, education, and Sufi calm rather than religious ritual or grandeur.
  • Architectural layout balanced seclusion for study and aesthetic pleasure.
  • Harmonized with local landscape and climate through material choice and design.

Unique Features

  • Among the few Mughal structures built for scientific study, not royal residence.
  • Integrates Islamic science, Sufi spirituality, and Mughal engineering.
  • Offers panoramic views of Dal Lake and surrounding mountains, chosen for observational clarity.
  • Terraces and sky views symbolize a metaphorical ascent to knowledge.
