Alamparai Fort

#fort #mughal #french #brick #civilengineering

Location & Era

Location: Alamparai, near Mahabalipuram
Built: 17th–18th century (Sambuvarayars, Nawabs, Mughals, French)

History

  • Built by the Sambuvarayars, later rebuilt and expanded by the Nawabs, Mughals, and French.[1][2][3][5][7]
  • Served as a bustling port and dockyard for trade in salt, silk, zari cloth, and ghee, exporting to Pondicherry and Portonova.[1][2][3][5][6][7]
  • Commissioned by Nawab Dost Ali Khan in the mid-1700s, later gifted to the French in 1750 for their support.[2][5][7]
  • Captured and demolished by the British in 1760; further damaged by the 2004 tsunami.[2][7]
  • Archaeological finds include coins, cannonballs, porcelain, and arms from the Nawab and French periods.[1][2][7]

Architecture

  • Rectangular plan (223m x 163m), tall freestanding brick and lime mortar walls (7m high), 9m bastion, 12 watchtowers, and tomb.[1][3][6][7]
  • Secret tunnel, rooms for soldiers, stables, and a dockyard extending into the sea.[1][2][3][5][6]
  • Canal for trade, rainwater tanks, and panoramic views of the Bay of Bengal.[1][6]
  • Walls and steps exhibit Mughal and French influences; red bricks and limestone coating are typical.[7]

Civil Engineering & Materials

  • Materials: Brick (high quartz content), lime mortar; bricks are low density, highly porous, and fired at low temperature, with compressive strength 11–18 MPa.[1][4][6]
  • Walls: Freestanding, tested to be as strong as or stronger than many modern bricks; require restoration due to erosion.[1][6]
  • Foundation: Facing the sea, rebuilt with boulders after the 2004 tsunami.[1]
  • Water Management: Canal for trade, rainwater tanks, and later a British-built canal for transport.[1][6]

Builders’ Thought Process

  • Bricks chosen for speed and local availability; design prioritized trade protection and sea defense.[1][6]
  • Adapted to coastal conditions and evolving needs of successive rulers.[1][2][3]
