Navratangarh Palace (Doisa Fort)

Nagvanshi Legacy, Gumla District, Jharkhand

Historical Background

  • Located in Gumla district, built in the 17th century by the Nagvanshi dynasty[1][3][4][5].
  • Served as the administrative and residential center for Nagvanshi rulers, especially under Durjan Sal and later Raghunath Shah[1][3][4][5].
  • Reflects the region’s feudal history, Mughal connections, and tribal influences[1][4][5].

Architecture

  • Blend of Rajput and tribal architectural styles, with a fortified palace complex[1][3][4].
  • Features courtyards, residential quarters, and a durbar hall with decorative arches[1][3].
  • Includes jharokhas (overhanging balconies) and chhatris (dome-shaped pavilions)[1][3].
  • Complex includes temples, tanks, watchtowers, and underground chambers[1][3][5].

Structural Design

  • Constructed on a hilltop, with defensive walls and a central courtyard layout[1][3][4].
  • Durbar hall uses corbelled arches and thick stone columns for support[1][3].
  • Jharokhas project from walls, supported by carved brackets, enhancing ventilation[1][3].
  • Multi-storeyed: originally up to 9 storeys, now mostly 3-5 remain above ground, with underground levels recently excavated[1][3][5].

Materials Used & Properties

  • Sandstone: Primary material for walls and arches, durable and locally available[1][3].
  • Lime Mortar: Binds stone blocks, offers flexibility and weather resistance[1][3].
  • Brick: Used in some interior structures, lighter but less durable than stone[1][3].
  • Wood: Used for doors and window frames, treated for durability[1][3].

RCC Comparison

  • RCC allows for larger, more flexible designs compared to rigid stone masonry.
  • Stone construction is less resistant to seismic activity than RCC’s tensile strength.
  • RCC requires less maintenance but lacks the historical and aesthetic value of stone.
Stability Features:
  • Hilltop location reduces flood risk and enhances defense[1][3][4].
  • Thick stone walls provide high compressive strength and stability[1][3].
  • Chhatris reduce wind load on the roof, improving structural integrity[1][3].
  • Deep foundations and underground chambers add to stability and defense[5].
Unique Features:
  • Integration of tribal motifs in carvings, reflecting Jharkhand’s indigenous culture[1][3].
  • Jharokhas for both aesthetic appeal and functional ventilation, a hallmark of Rajput-influenced design[1][3].
  • Secret tunnels and underground palace for defense and treasure storage[5].
  • Cluster of temples, tanks, and watchtowers within the fort complex[1][3][5].
