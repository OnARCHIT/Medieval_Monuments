Mahabodhi Temple

Bodh Gaya, Gaya District, Bihar, India — UNESCO World Heritage Site

Location Bodh Gaya, Gaya District, Bihar, India
24.6960° N, 84.9913° E
Dynasty Mauryan Empire (3rd century BCE, Ashoka); Gupta period additions (5th–6th century CE)
Deity Buddhist (Commemorates Gautama Buddha’s enlightenment)

Historical Background

Constructed by Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BCE, the Mahabodhi Temple marks the site where Gautama Buddha attained enlightenment under the Bodhi tree. Expanded during the Gupta period, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (2002) and a major Buddhist pilgrimage center, reflecting Bihar’s role as the birthplace of Buddhism.

References: Outlook Traveller, HIDMC, Wikipedia

Architecture

  • 55 m tall central spire with four smaller towers and a quadrangular base.
  • Gupta-era Buddhist aesthetics: symmetry, proportion, and intricate carvings.
  • Adjacent Bodhi tree and shrine are integral to the complex.

Reference: R.N. Misra, 2016

Building Materials & Properties

  • Brick: Core structure, locally sourced (~10–20 MPa compressive strength, high porosity).
  • Sandstone: Chunar sandstone cladding for carvings (~40–60 MPa, ~5–8% porosity).
  • Lime Mortar: Non-hydraulic, binder:aggregate ~1:3, with rice husk and jaggery for binding.
  • Brick: Lightweight, suited for large structures.
  • Sandstone: Durable, ideal for intricate carvings.
  • Lime Mortar: Breathable, flexible, suited to humid climates.

References: Ngoma, 2009, CE & CR, 2021

Builder Thought Process

Ashoka aimed to create a sacred monument to honor Buddha’s enlightenment, using brick for structural stability and sandstone for aesthetic appeal. Gupta builders enhanced the design with carvings, following Manasara principles for spiritual harmony. Lime mortar with organic additives ensured durability in Bihar’s humid climate.

Structural Design & Current Condition

  • Solid brick core with sandstone cladding, supported by a deep foundation.
  • The spire distributes loads evenly.
  • Well-preserved due to ASI restoration; minor weathering on sandstone.
  • Lime-based repairs address past cement-related damage.

Reference: INTACH Heritage at Risk Register

Comparison: Lime Masonry vs. RCC

  • Mahabodhi Temple (Lime-Masonry): Flexible, breathable, reduces moisture damage in humid climates.
  • RCC Structures: Higher strength (~20–50 MPa vs. ~5–10 MPa for lime mortar), but rigid and prone to cracking.
  • Advantage: Lime mortar’s porosity prevents water retention, unlike RCC.

Unique Features

  • Bodhi Tree: Descendant of the original tree under which Buddha attained enlightenment.
  • Carvings: Gupta-era reliefs depicting Buddha’s life.
